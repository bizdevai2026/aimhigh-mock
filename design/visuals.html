How to read these

  1. Current column lifts the SVG straight out of visuals.js. No filter, no flattering — that's exactly what a kid sees.
  2. Option A — Friendly illustrated commits to characters, soft colour, hand-drawn-feel outlines, and explanatory metaphors (rocket = fast, grass tufts = banks, tiny knights with swords). Bitesize-feel without copying any Bitesize asset.
  3. Option B — Technical refined stays diagrammatic but pushes hard on craft: gradients, callout lines extending outside the subject, axis units, tier chips, scale bars. Cobalt-premium feel that matches the chosen palette.
  4. Option C — AI hybrid uses an AI-generated painterly background under a clean SVG overlay. The backdrops here are hand-mocked stand-ins (CSS gradients + a noise filter); the real workflow is: you paste the listed prompt into your image-gen tool, save the PNG to assets/visuals/<key>.png, and I wire getVisual() to render the PNG with the SVG layer on top. Hybrid solves AI's three weaknesses (style drift, accuracy errors, garbled text) by keeping precision in the SVG layer where it can't hallucinate.
  5. Caveats on the AI route: every backdrop needs your eye for accuracy before going live (AI invents six-legged frogs and mislabelled organs); style consistency across 17 images takes a locked prompt template; outputs are originally created so copyright is clean, but the training of generative models on copyrighted material remains contested — for a personal-use revision app it's a non-issue, but worth knowing.
  6. For topics where a real photo would beat any illustration (e.g. real cells under a microscope, real river meanders from satellite), an additive Option D is to source CC-licensed photos from Wikimedia Commons / Pexels / Unsplash and pair them with the chosen overlay style. Doesn't conflict with A/B/C — it's a per-topic call.
